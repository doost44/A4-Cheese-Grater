GBDA 306 — Value Sensitive Design

A dual-mode cheese grater, designed for safety and mastery

A semester-long exploration of how Value Sensitive Design can transform an everyday kitchen tool into one that serves both children learning to cook and enthusiasts chasing flavour perfection.

01 — The Problem

One tool, two very different users

The standard box grater hasn't changed in decades. It works for adults but exposes blades at all times, provides no guidance, and prevents independent use by children. For enthusiasts, it offers limited texture control and no path to mastery. Core tension: the sharper a grater is, the better it performs — and the more dangerous it becomes.

Stakeholder 1

Children Cooking at Home

Kids aged 8–12 who want to participate in meal preparation. They already use graters in real kitchens — the design has to work with that reality.

Safety Independence Self-Development

"Kids can feel proud being part of the cooking process, but they're also the most likely to cut themselves or mishandle a grater." — A1

Stakeholder 2

Cheese & Cooking Enthusiasts

Home cooks who care deeply about flavour, texture, and technique. Grating is part of the sensory pleasure of cooking, not just a chore.

Mastery Hedonism Happiness

"They chase pleasure in flavour and texture, and they want mastery, because cooking is a skill they're trying to get better at." — A1

02 — Research & Validation

Grounding values in evidence

Values were validated through secondary research (S1) and a semi-structured interview (S2). Both shifted the designer's assumptions.

S1 — Children — Injury Data

The grater is not the safer option

Parents rate permission for cheese graters and sharp knives at nearly identical levels — both with a mean of 0.86 (SD 0.36). The grater sits right beside a sharp knife in real household practice.

Key insight: Kids already use graters. Safety can't mean preventing use — it means designing around real household behaviour.

S2 — Enthusiasts — Interview

Mastery through control & confidence

Semi-structured interview with an avid home cook and cheese enthusiast. The participant described cheese in emotional and sensory terms, confirming hedonism and happiness as core values.

"I get a bit nervous when the piece I'm grating gets small, and my fingers get close to the blade. But it doesn't stop me. I feel pretty brave when I grate something that close." — K, Interview Participant

Key insight: Mastery is expressed through control and confidence — accepting a bit of risk because it's part of doing something enjoyable.

03 — Value Tensions

Tensions aren't flaws — they're the problem worth solving

VSD teaches that value conflicts are not evidence of a flawed design. They're what make the problem meaningful. The challenge is negotiating them thoughtfully. (Friedman & Hendry, 2019; Van de Poel, 2013)

Safety vs. Mastery

S1 Safety VS S2 Mastery

Sharper blades and greater control improve the enthusiast's experience, but the same qualities make the tool dangerous for children. This can't be solved in a single universal configuration.

Resolution: A dual-mode design — two legitimate states of the same object. Safe Mode prioritizes protection; Pro Mode prioritizes performance. The tension becomes visible and intentional rather than hidden.

Independence vs. Safety

Independence VS Safety

Children want to participate and feel capable, but more freedom naturally introduces more risk. This is an internal tension within Stakeholder 1.

Resolution: Guided independence — the child acts, but the object sets guardrails. Enclosed feed paths, structured pushers, and visual cues let kids build skills without losing safety.

04 — Design Evolution

From box grater to dual-mode workstation

The biggest shift was moving from a handheld grater to a countertop workstation with two distinct modes. Instead of burying values in a manual, the object communicates them directly through its form and constraints.

Before — Standard Box Grater

Blades exposed at all times. No guidance or structure. Prevents independent use by children. Limited texture control. Values invisible in form.

→

After — GrateTogether

Dual-mode countertop workstation. Values embodied in physical form. Mode switching makes the value trade-off visible and intentional.

Safe Mode — Children (8–12)

Guided & Protected

  • Enclosed grating channel with shutter
  • Captive fingertip-safe pusher
  • End-stop preserves safe leftover stub
  • Bin-present interlock for operation
  • Green pictogram cues (load → push → collect)
  • Stable non-slip base

Pro Mode — Enthusiasts

Open & Precise

  • Fully exposed grating surface
  • Direct tactile control over angle & pressure
  • Swappable precision blade cartridges
  • Fine / coarse texture control
  • Brushed stainless & premium feel
  • Ergonomic 15° handle angle
Design Specification Details
Product: GrateTogether — Dual-mode countertop cheese grater workstation
Form: Upright trapezoid, wider base narrower top, soft radii. Height ≈ 2.2× body width.
Funnel: Rounded cylindrical/flared, centered, 45–50% of base width.
Handles: One per side, integrated ergonomic loop, dark charcoal slightly rubberized. Span 55–65% of body height.
Materials: Matte dark gray/charcoal body, muted green safe cues, brushed stainless grating plate, clear collection bin.
Mode Switch: Upper front control area, labeled SAFE (green) / PRO (red-dark), mechanically linked to shutter + pusher + bin latch.
Safe Mode: Shutter closed/latched, pusher captive along guided channel, bin must be inserted to operate. Pro exposure mechanically blocked.
Pro Mode: Shutter retracts, plate fully exposed, pusher parks or removed. Bin optional.

06 — Stakeholder Feedback & Refinement

Testing with real people

The prototype was shown to two stakeholders representing each user group. Their feedback shaped specific design refinements and validated core value propositions.

Stakeholder 01 · Cheese Enthusiast

Expert user feedback — structured interview

What worked

  • Exploded view of components is effective for communicating product architecture
  • All components clearly labelled and easy to identify at a glance
  • Interface reads as very clean and considered
  • Safe Mode → Pro Mode switching with the green-to-red visual transition feels authentic and communicates risk calibration intuitively
  • Top-right legend/label box adds context and is genuinely informative

Areas for refinement

  • User journey and primary call-to-action unclear — stakeholder was unsure what action the website was asking them to take
  • No ability to drag and configure different grater types onto the frame — desired more modularity and product customisation in the UI
  • Instructional copy at top of page visually collides with component labels, creating readability conflict

Refinements Made

CTA clarity Added primary action hierarchy and clearer user flow signposting
Drag interaction Flagged for V2 interactive configurator feature
Label overlap Adjusted z-index layering and type positioning to resolve collision

Stakeholder 02 · Child User · Age 7–10 · Stand-in Interview

Direct child research used a representative respondent per ethical research practice.

“I love the GrateTogether because I love helping my parents make dinner and my mum never lets me grate cheese because she thinks I will hurt myself. Now when we make my favourite dinner I get to help by grating the parmesan for spaghetti. My mum feels better about our new grater because she knows I will not accidentally hurt myself because of the Safe Mode. I love how easy it is too. I can grate a whole block of cheese super super fast.” — Stand-in respondent, age 9

Synthesis

The feedback validates the core safety proposition of GrateTogether’s Safe Mode. For this user group, the product removes a genuine point of household friction — children wanting to participate in food preparation while parents manage risk. The emotional outcome (inclusion, confidence, family collaboration) aligns directly with the product’s intended value.

Insight: Earned Autonomy

The “Pro Mode Aspiration” tension

The child expressed a desire to use Pro Mode but acknowledged her parents restrict access even to Safe Mode following a prior injury with a conventional grater. This reveals an important product narrative opportunity: Safe Mode is not just a safety feature — it is a trust-building gateway.

The progression from Safe → Pro Mode mirrors a real emotional journey of demonstrated responsibility and growing independence.

Design Opportunity

A future “graduated access” feature where Safe Mode usage builds toward unlocking Pro Mode — turning the grater into a tool that grows with the child.
